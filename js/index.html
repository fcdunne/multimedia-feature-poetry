Is the rise of Instagram poetry’s downfall?

2017 was poetry’s breakthrough year. Poetry sales rose by 10% and a record breaking amount of poetry books were sold. Kate Tempest played at Glastonbury and poetry was made cool. Yet the branch of poetry which may have contributed most to the heightened success was Instagram poetry. In fact, a new phrase was born, the Instapoet.

No one adorns the term better than Rupi Kaur- an Instagram poet with over two million followers. Her debut poetry book ‘Milk and Honey’, released in 2015, reached the top spot of the New York Times bestsellers list and finished second place on Amazon’s best sellers of 2017. In October, Kaur released her second collection ‘The Sun and Her Flowers’, which is already set to replace herself on the league tables in 2018. Although sales reflect her commercial popularity, controversy surrounds her name in the more traditional poetry world.

Three figures from Sheffield’s poetry scene give their opinion on what Instagram, and Instapoets, are doing to poetry.

Mark Greene is a Sheffield based poet. His work has been published in over 25 magazines internationally, including Now Then and Crannóg. Alongside commissions, he also publishes snippets of his work on his Instagram account, greenepoetlife. Since 2016, when he began to use Instagram to publicise his poetry, he’s managed to captivate a sizeable following of almost 7,700 users. So, as a rising Instagram poet, what does he think about the platform?

“I used to use Instagram as my personal account but I came home after a night out once and scribbled down a load of nonsense and I put it on Instagram and I got a lot of likes. So after that I thought I’d carry on posting. Initially I wasn’t aiming for anything, I just thought that in between writing my real poetry I would put up a few scribblings on Instagram. Then, the next minute people were emailing me asking if they could use my work for gifts and someone else wanted to use a poem for a video. People from Switzerland and as far away as Brazil have got in touch with me about using my work.

Initially, I built my following through other users liking and sharing my work but I think more about how I can use it now. So, if I post a poem, I’ll go through Instagram and like other poets work so they can click on me and see my account. I’ll hashtag and stuff, try and do about 30 hash tags and after I’ve put some up I’ll like other peoples’ posts. If people like you quite soon then you go to the home page, where you’re more likely to be seen by a wider audience. So, sometimes I’ll tag like ‘poetrygram’ or ‘bookstagram’ and when I’ve done that my posts have been in the top 9 posts on Instagram, and then you can tell that you get a lot more likes through.

That’s more of the marketing side coming out. But in a couple of years’ time, when I want to sell my work to a publisher, it’s going to be a lot easier to sell it if I can show them that I have a big following, if I’ve got like ten to 15,000 people following me.

I will admit though that a lot of the stuff you see on Instagram, it’s like motivational words rather than actual poetry. And that’s fine, if that’s what people want to read but I’m not too sure. Even the stuff I put up, I wouldn’t submit that to a poetry magazine. Rupi Kaur’s stuff, I wouldn’t actually class as poetry. It’s just a sentence, broken down. Although I wouldn’t mind making the money she makes.

I find on Instagram, you don’t want to put something up that people have to spend about half an hour figuring out. It’s more like ‘oh look at this poem. Read it. Like that. Next poem.’ Which I think Rupi Kaur is quite good at.

I wouldn’t go and buy a magazine of all of the best Instagram anthologies but if people enjoy doing it than that’s all that really matters.”
